A Field Guide

The Fender American Professional II Telecaster — How its Electronics Actually Work

Every component in the signal chain, how it shapes the sound, and how to fix the most common failure: the intermittent neck-position dropout.

01 Introduction

The Fender Telecaster has a deserved reputation as one of the simplest electric guitars ever made. There are only a handful of parts in the signal path, and almost all of them are passive: no transistors, no power supply, no microcontroller. A vibrating steel string moves through a magnetic field, that motion induces a tiny voltage in a coil of copper wire, and a few resistors, capacitors and a switch shape that voltage on its way to the output jack.

That simplicity is exactly why the Telecaster is a wonderful instrument to understand from first principles. By the end of this guide you should be able to point at every component in your guitar's control cavity, name what it does, sketch how it is wired, and — importantly for the problem you're chasing — reason about what could go wrong with it.

The specific instrument this guide describes is the Fender American Professional II Telecaster (model number prefix 011394, service manual PR5854). It uses two V-Mod II single-coil pickups, a 3-way lever switch, a 250 kΩ master volume with a treble bleed, and a 250 kΩ master tone with a 0.022 µF capacitor and a push-push switch that toggles the middle position between parallel and series wiring. We will walk through each of these in turn.

How to read this guide The early sections build up the vocabulary — what a pickup, a pot, a switch and a capacitor are, in isolation. The middle sections show how Fender wires them together. The last two sections are the practical payoff: a diagnostic walkthrough and a step-by-step repair procedure for the intermittent neck-position fault.

02 Signal Flow Overview

Before we open up any single component, here is the whole circuit at the highest possible level. The signal is born in the pickups and travels through five stages on its way to the amplifier:

  1. Pickups generate a small AC voltage (typically a few hundred millivolts) when strings vibrate above them.
  2. The 3-way switch chooses which pickup(s) feed the rest of the circuit.
  3. The volume pot attenuates the combined signal. The treble bleed across it preserves high frequencies as you turn down.
  4. The tone pot + capacitor form an adjustable low-pass filter, dumping high frequencies to ground when you roll the knob back.
  5. The output jack sends the signal — tip is hot, sleeve is ground — out the cable to the amplifier.

The push-push switch hidden inside the tone knob is a sixth element that is not in the main signal path itself; it just reroutes how the two pickups are combined when the 3-way switch is in its middle position. We will get to it in section 8.

03 The Pickups (V-Mod II)

A guitar pickup is, mechanically, almost the simplest electrical generator possible: six small bar magnets stood on end, with several thousand turns of very thin enameled copper wire wrapped around them. Above each magnet, a steel string is suspended.

When the string vibrates, it disturbs the magnetic field around the pole pieces. By Faraday's law of induction, a changing magnetic field through a coil of wire induces a voltage across that coil. The voltage waveform is an electrical mirror of the string's mechanical motion. That is the entire transducer — no power supply, no microphone, no amplification. Everything that comes after is just selecting and shaping that signal.

The American Pro II V-Mod II specs

Fender's V-Mod II Telecaster pickups are voiced specifically for this model. The factory specifications are:

PickupDC resistanceMagnetsPolarityLead wires
Neck 5.6 – 6.2 kΩ Alnico 5 & Alnico 2 (unbeveled) North Black, white, yellow
Bridge 7.2 – 7.8 kΩ Alnico 5 & Alnico 2 (unbeveled) South Black, red

Why two different magnet types?

Fender mixes Alnico 5 and Alnico 2 pole pieces within each pickup. Alnico 5 is the stronger magnet and tends to deliver a tight, clear, slightly aggressive top end; Alnico 2 is softer and gives a sweeter, more vocal midrange. By using both within the same pickup, the engineers can balance the response of individual strings — typically the wound strings sit under one magnet type and the plain strings under the other — without resorting to staggered pole heights.

Why opposite polarity between neck and bridge?

The neck pickup is wound and magnetized as north up; the bridge pickup is south up. Combined with reverse winding directions, this is the classic RWRP arrangement (reverse-wound, reverse-polarity). When both pickups are selected together (position 2 of the 3-way), any electromagnetic hum picked up from the surrounding environment — mostly 60 Hz from mains wiring — arrives at both coils in phase, but because the coils are wound in opposite directions, that hum signal cancels at the output. Meanwhile the actual string signal is reinforced because the magnetic polarity is also flipped, so it remains in phase. The result: position 2 is noticeably quieter (less hum) than positions 1 or 3.

Why three wires on the neck pickup?

The bridge pickup has just two leads — coil hot (black) and coil ground (red). The neck pickup has three:

  • Black — coil hot (start of winding).
  • White — coil cold (end of winding).
  • Yellow — metal cover/ground shield, kept on its own wire.

Keeping the cover ground separate from the coil cold is what makes the push-push series/parallel mod possible. In parallel mode the cold and the cover both go to ground, and the pickup behaves like any normal Tele neck pickup. In series mode (push-push engaged) the coil cold is lifted off ground and routed to the bridge pickup's hot lead instead, so the two coils end up wired in series. If the cover ground and coil cold were soldered together at the pickup, this switching would have no effect — the neck pickup's cold would always be grounded through the cover, and the series mod would silently behave like parallel.

If you ever re-solder the neck pickup Keep yellow (cover) and white (coil cold) on separate lugs. Joining them at the pickup defeats the push-push series mode permanently and is a common mistake.

04 The 3-Way Lever Switch

The 3-way lever switch (Fender part number 0992041000) is mechanically simple and electrically clever. Underneath the lever there are two parallel rows of solder lugs — the two poles — and a sliding wiper that rides on top of them. Each pole has a common lug at one end and three stage lugs along the side. As the lever moves, the wiper bridges the common to one or two of the stage lugs depending on the position.

A standard Fender Telecaster only uses one of the two poles to switch the pickups; the second pole is unused. (Strat-style 5-way switches use both poles, which is why the same part appears in many Fender guitars.)

Inside the switch — why it goes intermittent

The wiper is a small phosphor-bronze finger (or pair of fingers) held against the lug contacts by a leaf spring and held in detent positions by a notched rail. Three things eventually conspire against it:

  • Oxidation. Both the wiper and the lug contacts develop a thin oxide layer over time, especially in humid environments. Oxide is a poor conductor; signal drops out until the wiper scrapes through it again.
  • Spring fatigue. The leaf spring that pushes the wiper down weakens slowly. With less contact pressure, oxide buildup matters more, and any vibration can momentarily lift the wiper off the lug.
  • Solder-lug stress. Repeated soldering, or wires pulled tight, can crack the inside of a solder joint where you cannot see it. Wiggling the lever flexes the lug just enough to make and break the connection.

All three failure modes produce exactly the symptom you are experiencing: a position that usually works but cuts out, and that comes back when you wiggle the lever. Section 13 walks through how to confirm which one it is.

05 Master Volume Pot

A potentiometer (“pot”) is a three-terminal variable resistor. Inside the brass case is a curved strip of resistive material (usually a carbon-composite track) with a metal terminal at each end. A wiper, mounted on the rotating shaft, slides along that strip. The terminal connected to the wiper taps off whatever voltage is present at the wiper's current position on the strip.

How the volume pot attenuates the signal

In the Telecaster, terminal 1 (input) receives the signal coming out of the 3-way switch's common lug. Terminal 3 is grounded. The wiper (terminal 2) sends the output forward to the tone pot and the jack.

When the knob is fully clockwise, the wiper sits right at terminal 1. There is almost no resistance between input and output, and the full pickup signal comes through. As you turn the knob counter-clockwise, the wiper moves toward terminal 3 (ground). More and more of the signal is diverted to ground through the resistive track, and less reaches the output. At the fully closed position the wiper is at ground and you hear silence.

Audio taper vs linear taper, and why 250 kΩ

The Am Pro II uses a 250 kΩ audio (logarithmic) taper pot. “Audio taper” means the resistance changes logarithmically with rotation, not linearly. This matters because human hearing is itself logarithmic: a true linear pot would feel like all the volume change happens in the last quarter of the rotation. An audio taper feels smooth and even from 0 to 10 because it tracks how loud we perceive the change.

The total resistance value (250 kΩ) also affects tone, not just volume. Single coil pickups loaded with a lower-value pot lose more high frequencies to the loading effect. Fender uses 250 kΩ for single-coil instruments specifically because it tames the natural brightness of single coils. Humbucker-equipped guitars tend to use 500 kΩ (less loading) to keep them from sounding too dark.

06 Treble Bleed Circuit

There is one annoying side effect of the volume pot's loading interaction: as you turn down, you also lose treble, not just overall level. The combination of the pickup's inductance, the cable's capacitance, and the changing source impedance forms a low-pass filter whose corner frequency drops as the volume comes down. Roll from 10 to 5 on a stock guitar with no compensation and the tone goes muffled long before it gets quiet.

The Am Pro II ships with a small PCB (7711092000) soldered across two of the volume pot's lugs. This is the treble bleed: a small capacitor (typically a few hundred picofarads) often paired with a resistor, wired between terminal 1 (input) and terminal 2 (wiper / output) of the volume pot.

Capacitors pass high frequencies easily and block low frequencies. By placing a small cap between the pot's input and its wiper, you give the high frequencies a shortcut around the resistive track. As you turn the volume down, lows are attenuated normally but highs continue to leak through the cap, keeping the tone bright. The optional parallel resistor adjusts how much of the bass also gets through, fine-tuning the perceived flatness of the response across the rotation.

07 Master Tone Pot & Capacitor

The tone control is also a 250 kΩ audio-taper pot — same physical part as the volume pot, just wired differently and with a push-push switch built into the back of it (more on that in section 8). Instead of being placed in series with the signal, the tone pot is wired in parallel with it, with a 0.022 µF capacitor between the wiper and ground.

How it filters the signal

A capacitor's resistance to alternating current (its impedance) decreases as frequency increases. At very low frequencies (bass notes) the cap looks almost like an open circuit and very little signal flows through it to ground. At very high frequencies (cymbal-like sparkle, pick attack) the cap looks almost like a short circuit, providing a path for those frequencies to be dumped to ground.

The pot in series with the cap controls how easily those high frequencies reach the cap. With the tone knob full up (10), the pot is at its maximum 250 kΩ and very little signal makes it to the cap; the tone is bright. As you roll the knob down, the resistance drops, more high-frequency content flows through to ground, and the tone darkens. At zero, the pot is essentially zero ohms and the cap is shunting everything above its corner frequency directly to ground — the classic “woman tone” sound.

The choice of cap value sets the corner frequency. 0.047 µF (vintage Fender) starts rolling off lower in the spectrum, producing a darker, woollier sound at zero. 0.022 µF — what Fender uses on the Am Pro II — rolls off higher, leaving more midrange present even with the tone knob all the way down.

08 Push-Push Series/Parallel Switch

The tone pot on the Am Pro II is a special part (Fender 7713119000): it is a 250 kΩ audio-taper pot with a push-push latching switch built into the body. Pushing the knob down once latches the switch in one state; pushing it again releases it back to the other state. The switch is mechanically independent of the pot's rotation — you can change the tone setting without affecting the switch state, and vice versa.

The switch itself is a DPDT — double-pole, double-throw. It has six lugs arranged as two independent switches that move together: each “pole” (an input lug) can be connected to one of two “throws” (output lugs) at any time. In the Am Pro II this DPDT is used to reroute the connection between the two pickups when the 3-way is in position 2.

Position 2: parallel mode (push-push down)

With the push-push down — the default — the switch routes both pickups through the 3-way switch in the conventional manner. Both pickup hots end up tied together at the 3-way's common lug; both pickup grounds (and the neck cover ground) go to the chassis ground. The result is the classic “both on” Tele tone. Electrically the two pickups are wired in parallel: their outputs sum, but the effective output impedance is roughly halved. The sound is balanced and a bit scooped in the mids, with good clarity.

Position 2: series mode (push-push up)

With the push-push up, the DPDT does two things at once:

  1. Lifts the neck pickup's coil cold (white) off ground.
  2. Routes that lifted lead to the bridge pickup's hot lead instead.

Now the signal path is: bridge pickup ground → bridge pickup hot → (through DPDT) → neck pickup cold → through the neck coil → neck pickup hot → into the 3-way switch → volume. The two coils are stacked end-to-end: one drives the next, in series.

Why series sounds the way it does

In parallel, the two pickups' voltages average and their impedances drop. In series, their voltages add and their impedances add. The audible effects are:

  • More output. Roughly twice the voltage swing, so you hit the amp harder.
  • Thicker midrange. Doubled impedance shifts the resonant peak of the pickup/cable system down into the mids, fattening the sound.
  • Less top-end sparkle. Because of that resonance shift, the very top frequencies are slightly tamed.

The result is a Tele middle position that approximates the punch and warmth of a humbucker without changing pickups. It is still hum-cancelling, because the RWRP relationship between the two coils is preserved.

09 Output Jack

The output is a standard 1/4" mono “phone” jack (Fender 0021956049). It has two contacts: the tip contact, which carries the hot signal, and the sleeve contact, which is connected to the chassis ground.

When you insert a plug, two things happen mechanically:

  • A spring contact inside the jack presses against the metal sleeve of the plug, completing the ground connection.
  • A second spring contact, normally arched away, touches the metal tip of the plug, completing the hot connection.

The shape of those internal spring contacts is what determines whether the jack feels “tight” or sloppy. Output jacks fail in essentially the same ways the 3-way switch does: the spring contacts lose tension, oxidize, or the solder lugs work loose. If a guitar “cuts out when you wiggle the cable,” the jack is the suspect; if it cuts out when you operate the pickup selector, the 3-way is.

10 Grounding Scheme

Every component in the guitar that is supposed to be at ground potential needs to arrive at the same physical point with as little wire and as few hops as possible. Fender does this by using the back of the volume pot's brass case as a star ground — the central hub that every other ground wire returns to.

  • The neck pickup's cold (white) lead and cover ground (yellow) terminate on the volume pot back (or are routed through the push-push DPDT first).
  • The bridge pickup's cold (red) lead terminates on the volume pot back.
  • The 3-way switch's ground lug runs to the volume pot back.
  • The tone pot's ground lug and case sit at the same potential because both pots' cases are bonded.
  • The output jack's sleeve lug runs to the volume pot back.
  • A wire from the bridge plate (under the bridge, in contact with the strings) runs into the control cavity and grounds to the volume pot back.

That last wire is critical for two reasons. First, it grounds you, the player, whenever you touch the strings, which dramatically reduces the hum your body picks up. Second, it grounds the bridge plate itself so it can't act like a small antenna radiating noise into the pickups.

Safety note about strings-as-ground Because your body is connected to the amp's ground via the strings, a faulty amp with a wiring problem (or one with a polarity-inverting plug into a damaged outlet) can put dangerous voltage on the strings. If you ever feel a tingle on your lips when they touch a microphone while playing, stop and have the amp's ground checked.

13 Troubleshooting the Neck Dropout

Now to your specific symptom: in position 3 (neck pickup only), the sound sometimes cuts out, and wiggling the toggle restores it. This is one of the most common Telecaster faults, and the “wiggling fixes it” clue narrows down the suspect list almost immediately. The fault has to be somewhere mechanical that the lever physically flexes when you wiggle.

Likely causes, ordered by probability

  1. Worn or oxidized contacts on the 3-way lever switch at the neck-position lug. By a wide margin the most common cause. Years of playing flex the spring, oxide builds up on both wiper and lug, and at some point the contact becomes inconsistent. Wiggling momentarily scrubs the oxide and restores conduction.
  2. Cold or cracked solder joint at the neck pickup hot lug on the switch. A solder joint that was never properly heated when assembled can develop a hairline crack that opens and closes as the lug flexes. Looks shiny on the outside but is electrically intermittent.
  3. Broken or intermittent neck pickup hot lead. The thin pickup lead can fracture inside its insulation, especially right where it exits the pickup or where it threads through the body cavity. Less common than the switch itself but worth checking.
  4. Loose switch mounting screws. If the two screws holding the switch to the control plate are loose, the whole switch body can shift slightly under the lever, fooling the mechanism into thinking it's in a different position than the lever actually is. Easy to check, easy to fix.
  5. Failed pickup coil (least likely given your symptoms). A truly broken coil winding usually fails permanently — you would not be able to wiggle the toggle and bring sound back, because the lever has nothing to do with the coil itself. Mentioned only for completeness.

Diagnostic procedure with a multimeter

Set your multimeter to continuity mode (it will beep when resistance is below a few ohms) for the switch tests, and to resistance mode (kilo-ohm range) for the pickup test. Make sure the guitar is unplugged from the amp.

  1. Open the control cavity. Slacken the strings (or remove them entirely if you prefer), then unscrew the four small Phillips screws holding the control plate to the body. Lift the plate out gently — the pickup leads run underneath it and you don't want to yank them. Flip the plate over so you can see the underside of the switch and pots.
  2. Identify the lugs. Match what you see against figure 11.1. The 3-way switch should have wires on three of its lugs along one side: bridge stage, neck stage, and the common lug at the end. Confirm which is which by tracing the colored wires back to their pickups.
  3. Test the suspect path. Put the 3-way switch in position 3 (neck). Touch one multimeter probe to the neck pickup's hot lug at the switch and the other probe to the volume pot's input lug. The meter should beep continuously. Now wiggle the toggle lever. If the beep cuts out and comes back, the switch is conclusively the failure point.
  4. Test the pickup itself. Move the meter to resistance mode and probe directly across the neck pickup leads (black to white). You should read a stable value in the 5.6 – 6.2 kΩ range. Tap and gently flex the pickup leads while watching the reading. If it jumps to “OL” (open / no continuity) intermittently, the pickup lead is fractured.
  5. Inspect solder joints. With good light and ideally a magnifier, look at every solder joint on the switch lugs. Healthy joints look smooth, slightly shiny and conical, with the wire fully wetted by solder. Cold or cracked joints look dull, grainy, ball-shaped, or have a visible ring around the wire where it enters the blob.
  6. Check the mounting screws. Try to wiggle the switch body itself relative to the control plate. There should be no movement at all. If the switch shifts, snug up the two mounting screws (carefully — they thread directly into the switch's plastic body and will strip if overtightened).

In the overwhelming majority of cases, step 3 will identify the switch as the culprit and you can move directly to the repair guide below. If step 3 passes cleanly but the problem still occurs in normal play, the issue is upstream (steps 4–6) or the problem is intermittent enough that you didn't catch it during the test — try again after letting the guitar sit for a while, since temperature and humidity affect oxidation behavior.

14 Repair Guide: 3-Way Switch

Before you start Unplug the guitar. Have the amp off and disconnected. Solder fumes are unpleasant — work in a ventilated area. Keep the iron's tip away from pickup magnets and from your fingers; soldering irons run at 350 °C and burn instantly.

Tools and materials

  • Small Phillips-head screwdriver (PH1)
  • Soldering iron, 25–40 W, with a clean fine tip
  • Solder — 60/40 leaded rosin-core (easiest to work with) or a quality lead-free rosin-core
  • Desoldering braid or a desolder pump
  • Needle-nose pliers and small tweezers
  • A digital multimeter
  • Helping-hands or a soft work surface so the body isn't damaged
  • Track A only: DeoxIT D5 contact cleaner
  • Track B only: Fender 3-way switch P/N 0992041000 (or any standard CRL-style equivalent)

Track A — Clean and re-tension the existing switch (try this first)

If the diagnostic in section 13 confirmed an intermittent contact at the switch but everything looks intact, you can often save the original switch with a 10-minute clean.

  1. Slacken the strings; unscrew the control plate; flip it carefully so the wires aren't pulled tight.
  2. Take a few sharp photos of the wiring as it currently is, from multiple angles. This is a free insurance policy in case anything moves.
  3. Turn the DeoxIT D5 nozzle so the spray comes out as a small jet rather than a wide mist. Aim into the open side of the switch where the wiper rides on the lugs and give it a brief, controlled spray.
  4. Work the lever back and forth through all three positions twenty to thirty times. The cleaner dissolves oxide and the mechanical motion scrubs the contacts clean.
  5. Let the residue evaporate for a few minutes. Wipe up any drips with a clean cloth.
  6. Re-test with the multimeter as in section 13, step 3. The continuity beep should now be rock solid even when you wiggle the lever.
  7. If solid, reassemble. Snug the mounting screws gently; do not overtighten.

If after cleaning the symptom returns within a few weeks, the contact pressure has genuinely failed and you should move on to Track B. Cleaning a switch with weak contact-finger spring tension is at best a temporary fix.

Track B — Replace the 3-way switch

Replacement is straightforward but methodical. The risk is mis-wiring the new switch; photos and labeling prevent this.

  1. Photograph everything. Take close-up photos of the existing switch wiring from multiple angles before you touch anything. You will want these as reference even if you trust the diagram in figure 14.1.
  2. Label each wire. Wrap a small piece of masking tape around each wire a few centimeters from its solder joint. Write “neck hot”, “bridge hot”, “to volume” (the common lug) and so on. Trust nothing to memory.
  3. Desolder the wires. Heat each joint until the solder flows, then use desoldering braid (press the braid against the joint and apply the iron on top of it) to wick the solder away. The wire should lift free easily; if it resists, apply more heat — never pull hard.
  4. Remove the old switch. Two small Phillips screws hold the switch to the control plate. Keep the screws — you'll reuse them.
  5. Mount the new switch. Orient it the same way the old one came out (the lever-throw direction matters!). Tighten the mounting screws snugly but not crushingly — the switch body is plastic and will strip.
  6. Pre-tin the lugs. Touch a small amount of fresh solder to each lug you plan to use, just enough to coat it. This makes the actual joints much easier.
  7. Solder wires lug-for-lug. Working from your photo, attach each wire to its corresponding lug on the new switch. The connections you need are:
    • Bridge pickup hot → bridge stage lug (figure 14.1, leftmost on used pole)
    • Neck pickup hot → neck stage lug (rightmost on used pole)
    • A short jumper wire from the common lug → volume pot input (lug 1)
    • Switch ground (the metal frame of the switch) → volume pot back, via a short ground wire
    For each joint: insert the wire through the lug's hole, heat the joint with the iron, feed in just enough solder for it to flow into a smooth concave fillet around the wire, then remove the iron and hold the wire still until the solder freezes (a couple of seconds). A good joint is shiny, smooth, and conical.
  8. Verify before closing up. With the multimeter in continuity mode, confirm:
    • Position 1 (bridge): continuity from bridge hot → volume in; no continuity from neck hot → volume in.
    • Position 2 (both): continuity from both bridge hot and neck hot → volume in. Push-push down vs up should change which other paths are made; verify against figure 11.1.
    • Position 3 (neck): continuity from neck hot → volume in; no continuity from bridge hot → volume in.
    • Wiggle the lever in each position. Continuity should not flicker.
  9. Reassemble. Tuck the wires back into the cavity, drop the control plate into place taking care not to pinch any wires, and reinstall the four control-plate screws. Re-string and tune up.
  10. Real-world test. Plug into the amp and play through every position and every push-push state. Pay particular attention to the formerly-faulty neck position; it should be silent (no dropouts) even with vigorous lever wiggling.
If you hear a buzz or hum after the repair Most likely a ground wire is loose. Re-open the cavity and confirm: pickup grounds, switch ground, jack sleeve and bridge plate ground all terminate at the volume pot back. The buzz disappearing when you touch the strings means the bridge ground wire is intact; if even touching the strings doesn't help, that wire has come loose.

15 Glossary & Further Reading

Glossary

ACAlternating current. The pickup output, like all audio signals, is AC.
AlnicoAluminum-Nickel-Cobalt magnet alloy. Numbered grades (2, 5, etc.) describe magnetic strength and tonal character.
CapacitanceA component's ability to store electrical charge. Measured in farads (F); guitar caps are in microfarads (µF) or nanofarads (nF).
ContinuityAn electrical connection of essentially zero resistance. A continuity test is the multimeter's way of checking that two points are wired together.
DPDTDouble-pole, double-throw. A switch with six lugs that can route two independent signals to one of two destinations each.
ImpedanceAC-equivalent of resistance; depends on frequency. Pickup output impedance interacts with cable capacitance to shape tone.
InductanceA coil's resistance to changes in current. Pickups are inductive, which is why they form filters with capacitive cables.
Low-pass filterA circuit that lets low frequencies through and attenuates high frequencies. The tone control is one.
Parallel wiringTwo coils whose hots are tied together and whose grounds are tied together. Output voltages average; impedances drop.
Pole / throwSwitch terminology. A “pole” is one independent switching contact; a “throw” is one of the destinations it can connect to.
Potentiometer (pot)A three-terminal variable resistor used as either a volume control (voltage divider) or, in the Tele tone control, as a variable shunt.
RWRPReverse Wound, Reverse Polarity. Two pickups with opposing winding direction and opposing magnetic polarity, so that combining them cancels hum.
Series wiringTwo coils stacked end-to-end: one's output drives the next's input. Voltages add; impedances add.
Star groundA grounding scheme where every ground wire returns to a single physical point, eliminating ground loops and noise.
Treble bleedA small capacitor (sometimes with a resistor) wired across a volume pot's input and wiper to keep high frequencies present as the volume is turned down.
V-Mod IIFender's pickup line introduced with the American Professional II series. Mixes Alnico 5 and Alnico 2 magnets within each pickup for tuned per-string response.
